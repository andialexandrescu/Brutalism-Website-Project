Karaburma Housing Tower Building, Belgrade

The Karaburma residential housing tower is often referred to by locals at the Toblerone Tower, a nod to the triangular shapes incorporated into the design of the tower. It was designed by Rista Šekerinski and completed in 1963.

The apartment block stands in the Belgrade neighborhood of Karaburma, near Pancevo bridge and is one of several landmarks in the former Yugoslav capital built in the brutalist architectural style.

Alexandra and Ainsworth Estate, London

The Alexandra Road estate (officially the Alexandra and Ainsworth estate, but often referred to as Rowley Way, the name of its main thoroughfare) is a housing estate in the London Borough of Camden, North West London, England. It was designed in a brutalist style in 1968 by Neave Brown of Camden Council's Architects Department. Construction work commenced in 1972 and was completed in 1978. It is constructed from site-cast, board-marked white, unpainted reinforced concrete. Along with 520 apartments, the site also includes a school, community centre, youth club, heating complex, and parkland.

Alexandra Road is London terrace house with the design of high-density public housing. Alexandra road represents the application of the terraced theme on an enormous scale.

The long site is consisted of three parallel rows of dwellings. Two rows of terraced apartments are aligned along the tracks with the higher 8 story stepped building designed to block the noise of the trains. A lower, 4-story block runs along the other side of a continuous public walkway that serves both terraced rows of buildings. The third row of building, along the southern edge of the site, parallels another public walkway between this row and the existing earlier buildings of the Ainsworth Estate and defines an open public park between the second and third row of dwellings.

Double Helice, Paris

Another alluring construction out in Noisy-le-Grand, this multi-storey parking lot is known as the 'double helix' for its photo-worthy twists and bends. Like a crash-landed concrete UFO, Ludovic Maillard’s ’70s masterpiece wouldn’t look out-of-place in a sci-fi blockbuster.

A place of happiness, it's a deserted, giant architecture in beautiful aged concrete, with texture and imperfections. There are 4000 parking spaces spread over four levels served by two helical ramps. Like a space station attached to the Arcades shopping center, the parking lot is intended for residents as well as customers of the shops, office employees, and RER users, who also pass through it to quickly reach the Abraxas Spaces.

Grunwaldzki Estate, Wroclaw

Plac Grunwaldzki in Wroclaw, Poland, is renowned for its striking skyline dominated by the iconic Plac Grunwaldzki skyscrapers, also known as "Sedesowce" or "Manhattan." This architectural marvel comprises a complex of six modernist high-rises that stand as a testament to the city's architectural ingenuity.

Constructed between 1970 and 1973, these towering structures soar to heights ranging from 54 to 60 meters, casting an imposing presence against the urban backdrop. Designed in a distinctive Brutalist and modernist style by architect Jadwiga Grabowska-Hawrylak, the Plac Grunwaldzki Estate embodies both the ethos of its era and a timeless elegance that continues to captivate observers to this day.

Catedral Metropolitana Nossa Senhora Aparecida, Brasilia

The Cathedral of Brasília, also known as the Catedral Metropolitana de Brasília, serves as the principal Roman Catholic cathedral in Brasília, Brazil, and acts as the seat of the Archdiocese of Brasília. Designed by the esteemed Brazilian architect Oscar Niemeyer and engineered by Joaquim Cardozo, the cathedral was completed and consecrated on May 31, 1970.

This architectural marvel is characterized by its hyperboloid structure, composed of 16 colossal concrete columns, each weighing 90 tons. Surrounding the cathedral's entrance plaza are four bronze sculptures, standing at 2.5 meters tall, representing the four Evangelists and crafted by sculptors Alfredo Ceschiatti and Dante Croce in 1968.

Round Block, Bucharest

The Round building, locally known as "Blocul Rotund," stands as a distinctive landmark in Lujerului square, situated within the Militari neighborhood of Bucharest, Romania. Constructed in the late 1970s, this architectural gem bears the signature of architect A. Badescu.

Its circular design sets it apart from the surrounding structures, contributing to its unique charm and identity. As a testament to the architectural trends of its era, the Round building reflects the modernist principles prevalent during its construction period.

Hotel Salyut, Kiev

Hotel Salyut is a hotel on a green hill at the right side of the Dnieper River, it is surrounded by lush greenery of the Pechersk District which is the administrative and cultural center of Kiev. Designed in 1976, and built by 1984 by the Ukrainian architect Abraham Moiseevich Miletsky, together with N. Slohotska and Vladimir Shechenko, features 88 comfortable guestrooms of different categories with wonderful views of the UNESCO protected Kyivo-Pecherska Lavra and also the Dnieper River.

In the beginning the project of construction for this interesting building was supported by the Communist party leadership, however, after the collective refused to add the curator from the party on the list of authors, the construction project slowly began to decelerate. This is the main reason why the hotel did not complete as initially planned, which at the beginning was supposed to be a skyscraper designed with application of interesting and cutting edge construction for that time. Also the budget for the construction entailed problems, making the building to have less floors than first planned where the plans had to change with the ongoing problems.

Torre Velasca, Milan

The Torre Velasca is a skyscraper built in the 1950s by the BBPR architectural partnership, in Milan, Italy. The tower is part of the first generation of Italian modern architecture, while still being part of the Milanese context in which it was born, to which also belongs the Milan Cathedral and the Sforza Castle. It is located in the city centre of Milan, near the Duomo (Milan Cathedral) and the headquarters of the University of Milan.

The tower, measuring 75 metres (246 ft) in height to the roof with an additional 23-metre-tall (75 ft) antenna spire (total 98 m or 322 ft), has a peculiar and characteristic mushroom-like shape. It stands out in the city skyline, made of domes, buildings and other towers. Its structure recalls the Lombard tradition, made of medieval fortresses and towers, each having a massive profile. In such fortresses, the lower parts were always narrower, while the higher parts were propped up by wood or stone beams.

At the same time, BBPR in this building satisfied the functional needs of space: narrower surfaces on the ground, wider and more spacious ones on the top floors. The town planning laws, then, imposed specific volumes (depending on the buildings' purpose); in this tower, the latter were the mixed functions of residential and commercial use.

Dom na Begovoy, Moscow

In the bronze medal position for the largest residential complex along Moscow's bustling Third Transport Ring stands the formidable "House on the Runaway." Nestled along Khoroshevsk Highway, at house number 12, building 1, this architectural behemoth boasts an impressive tally of 1,947 apartments distributed across its expansive structure. With six grand entrances beckoning residents and visitors alike, the House on the Runaway ascends majestically to a towering height of 37 floors, dominating the city skyline with its commanding presence. Affectionately known as "Dom na Begovoy" among locals, this monumental edifice stands as a testament to Moscow's urban landscape, offering a myriad of living spaces within its formidable walls.
